Dr. J. Thomas August

Founder + Chief Science Officer

Dr. J. Thomas August

Dr. August is well-known internationally for his work with RNA viruses. A graduate of Stanford University School of Medicine, he served on the faculties of the Royal Infirmary, University of Edinburgh, Scotland; Harvard Medical School; and the Stanford University School of Medicine where he was appointed Assistant Professor and Markle Scholar in Medical Sciences; New York University School of Medicine as an Associate Professor of Medicine and Microbiology; and Albert Einstein College of Medicine as the Siegfried Ullmann Professor of Molecular Biology , and Chair of the Department of Molecular Biology and the Division of Biology in. He moved to The Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine as Director of the Department of Pharmacology and Molecular Sciences. His research associations also included appointments to the Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory of Quantitative Biology; Career Scientist, Health Research Council of the City of New York; Fellow, John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation, Visiting Professor,  Biochemistry, and Visiting Fellow, Balliol College, Oxford University; Recipient of Johnson and Johnson Focused Giving Award; Fellow, American Association for the Advancement of Science; Director, Division of Biological Science, Johns Hopkins Singapore; Adjunct Professor of Medicine, National University of Singapore; University Distinguished Service Professor, JHUSOM; and an NIAID R37 Merit Award.

Dr. August’s research over his entire career has centered mainly on the molecular biology of RNA viruses and their replication. In 1985, this included the discovered the lysosome-associated membrane glycoprotein (LAMP), and the co-localization of protein in cellular compartments that also continued the major histocompatibility protein II that transports pathogen antigens to the cellular immune response system. From that time he has devoted his research to the design of DNA vaccines that contain selected pathogen proteins encoded as chimeras of the LAMP molecule. The LAMP technology, licensed by Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine to another company for the development of allergen vaccines, was recently licensed to a major pharmaceutical company for $300 million, resulting in the largest license payment in the history of Johns Hopkins University.

In recognition of his record in the development of DNA vaccines, Johns Hopkins granted Pharos the worldwide license to the LAMP patents for flaviviruses and influenza, two of his major contributions.

David Wise

Chief Executive Officer

David Wise

David W. Wise has been deeply involved in the medtech innovation and startup community in Baltimore for a number of years, principally as the Venture Advisor on the direct investment program by the Abell Foundation. In that capacity he has been involved in two Series A rounds and one Series B round of investments, as well as five seed stage investments and three loans. He is a member of the Advisory Board of Betamore, a technology incubator. He also served on investment review committees for Accelerate Baltimore, bwtech, the VOLT Fund and The Fischell Department of Bioengineering at the University of Maryland in College Park. He is a member of the Association for Corporate Growth and sits on the Bioscience subcommittee of the Greater Baltimore Committee.

From 2005 to 2014 he was the President and Chief Executive Officer of The Genetics and IVF Institute (GIVF) in Fairfax, Virginia, an international women’s healthcare company with operations in China. Altogether he has almost sixteen years of C-Level experience having previously been Chief Operating Officer of the Lang Companies in Delafield, Wisconsin which sold the company to a private equity firm, and as Chief Financial and Administrative Officer for the Centennial Companies in Tysons Corner, Virginia where he had been brought in to manage a workout of the company’s $450 million in debt. He was also the Vice President for Corporate Development at BDM International, a Fortune 1000 company in which the Carlyle Group was the principal investor. At BDM he participated on the IPO team, completed numerous acquisitions including an international transaction, and co-managed the sale of the company to TRW in a $1 billion transaction.

Mr. Wise serves on the board of the Maryland Humanities Council and the advisory board of Lawrenceville Plasma Physics. He is a member of the US Navy Strategy Discussion Group and of the International Institute of Strategic Studies (London).

He graduated with highest honors from the University of Dayton in 1975 and in 1982 received an MALD degree in international business from The Fletcher School of Law & Diplomacy, which is co-administered by Tufts and Harvard Universities.

Dr. Hai-Quan Mao

Lead Scientist, Vaccine Delivery Systems

Dr. Hai-Quan Mao

Dr. Mao is working with Pharos under the terms of a company-funded sponsored research agreement with Johns Hopkins University where he is a professor in the departments of Materials Science and Engineering and Biomedical Engineering at Johns Hopkins University, and currently holds a joint appointment in the Translational Tissue Engineering Center at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine. He also serves as an associate director of Johns Hopkins Institute for NanoBioTechnology.

Dr. Mao’s research is focused on engineering novel nanomaterials for molecular therapy and for regenerative medicine applications. He received the Cygnus Award for Outstanding Work in Drug Delivery from the Controlled Release Society in 1997 for his research on gene delivery and received the Capsugel Awards for Outstanding Research in Innovative Aspects of Controlled Drug Release in 1998 and 2001 for his work on DNA vaccine delivery. He was the recipient of the Young Investigator Award at National University of Singapore in 2002, the National Science Foundation Faculty CAREER Award in 2008 for his work on artificial matrix for stem cell engineering. He was elected Fellow of Royal Society of Chemistry in 2014, and member of the National Academy of Inventors in 2015.

Dr. Mao has served as an expert panelist on a number of scientific review panels for the National Science Foundation Biomaterials Program, National Institutes of Health, and California Institute for Regenerative Medicine, New York State Stem Cell Program, and other programs. Mao’s research is focused on (1) developing nanoparticle systems to deliver plasmid DNA, siRNA and other therapeutic agents and (2) creating nanofiber matrix platforms as an artificial stem cell niche to direct stem cell expansion or differentiation. He has published 115 peer-reviewed articles and 18 issued US patents and more than a dozen of provisional patent applications.
